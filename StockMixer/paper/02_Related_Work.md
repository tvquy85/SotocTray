## 2. Related Work

### 2.1. Traditional and Deep Portfolio Optimization
Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT), introduced by Markowitz [Markowitz, 1952], forms the foundational bedrock of quantitative asset allocation by formalizing the trade-off between expected return and variance. While MPT and its subsequent extensions (e.g., the Black-Litterman model) provided robust mathematical guarantees, they relied heavily on strict assumptions regarding normal return distributions and stationary covariance matrices—assumptions frequently violated in real-world financial markets.

With the advent of Deep Learning, the paradigm shifted toward predictive and Reinforcement Learning (RL) based portfolio management. DeepTrader [Wang et al., 2021] utilized spatial-temporal graph neural networks to model asset correlations, while AlphaStock [Wang et al., 2019] integrated attention mechanisms with RL to rank assets. More recently, sequence-to-sequence models and Transformer-based architectures have been heavily adapted to capture long-term financial dependencies. However, these models inherently operate under the assumption that the financial environment's complexity can be fully captured by historical feature mapping, often neglecting the friction of real-world trading.

### 2.2. Transaction Cost and Risk Constraints
The primary obstacle in transitioning a theoretical trading algorithm to a live fund is the erosion of capital via transaction costs (turnover friction) and market impact. Traditional approaches address this by incorporating a fixed L1-norm penalty on weight changes between consecutive time steps. However, as demonstrated by the catastrophic drawdowns of rigid DRL algorithms during the 2020 COVID-19 crash or the crypto-winters, a static turnover penalty cannot adapt to rapid regime shifts.

Simultaneously, mitigating risk in deep financial models often involves clipping the gross reward or using the Sharpe Ratio as a direct optimization target. Yet, maximizing the Sharpe ratio penalizes upside volatility equally with downside volatility. Sortino-inspired RL models introduced downside variance penalties, but again, these were deployed as static hyperparameter weights. The inability of the agent to organically decide *when* to be risk-averse versus *when* to chase aggressive alpha remains a profound gap in the literature.

### 2.3. Meta-Learning and Dynamic Weighting
Meta-learning and dynamic loss weighting have seen substantial success in addressing label noise and class imbalance in computer vision (e.g., Meta-Weight-Net). In time-series forecasting, models have utilized gating networks to route predictions based on volatility. However, the direct application of context-conditioned meta-learning to co-adapt financial penalty functions—specifically linking transaction costs and downside variance to an inferred "market regime"—is virtually unexplored. Our work fills this gap by extending the static loss landscape of models like TC-StockMixer into a dynamic, regime-aware manifold.
